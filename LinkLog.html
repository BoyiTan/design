Case Study 02

LinkLog

Designing Continuity for Long-Term Social Prescribing Follow-ups

Social prescribing does not end after referral. Link Workers need to understand each older adult’s evolving context across 3-, 6-, and 12-month follow-ups.

LinkLog explores how a lightweight follow-up support system can help Link Workers prioritize clients, understand context, and document interactions without adding unnecessary documentation burden.

Follow-up Workflow Overview
A system view connecting the priority queue, client profile, context filter, and interaction log into one continuous follow-up workflow.
Problem

Follow-up context was scattered across spreadsheets, notes, paper records, and memory.

Link Workers needed to understand each client’s current situation before reaching out, but the information required to do so was difficult to retrieve quickly.

Direction

How might we help Link Workers maintain long-term client context?

The design focused on making client history, follow-up status, and next steps visible before, during, and after each interaction.

Challenge

Structure the workflow without replacing human judgement.

The system needed to support consistency and continuity while remaining flexible enough for different SALC practices.

Solution Preview

A follow-up support system that helps Link Workers prioritize clients, prepare with context, log interactions, and maintain continuity across long-term social prescribing journeys.

Solution Preview Screens
Four core screens show how a Link Worker moves from queue triage to client context review, live call logging, and next-step scheduling.
Research Evidence

The service problem was longitudinal, not transactional.

Based on previous cohort research, service blueprint analysis, workflow mapping, sprint discussion, and prototype critique, our team identified follow-up as a key gap in the Links2Wellbeing service experience.

Follow-up is ongoing

3-, 6-, and 12-month follow-ups require workers to understand how each client’s needs and participation change over time.

Context is fragmented

Client history may live across spreadsheets, notes, paper records, and individual memory, making preparation harder.

Records need both structure and story

Structured fields support consistency, while freeform notes preserve the human context behind each interaction.

The Insight

The problem was not simply missing data.
It was the lack of a clear structure for turning scattered follow-up records into usable client context.

Design Response

Designing continuity before automation

Instead of designing a fully automated or AI-driven system, I focused on a lightweight structure that supports human judgement while reducing memory reliance.

Simple structure over complex automation
Context before action
Structured input with space for narrative notes
Reduce memory reliance, not human judgement
01

Priority-Based Follow-up Dashboard

Link Workers needed to know who required attention first, and why. I designed a dashboard that surfaces overdue follow-ups, due-this-week clients, contact attempts, follow-up type, and priority level.

Why it matters

The dashboard becomes a workflow entry point rather than a database view, helping workers move from “all clients” to “who needs attention now.”

Priority Dashboard
The dashboard groups overdue, due-this-week, and high-priority clients so workers can start with the most time-sensitive follow-ups.
Client Profile
A single profile keeps intake details, interaction history, and 3-, 6-, and 12-month follow-up stages visible in one place.
02

Client Profile with Longitudinal Follow-up Tabs

Social prescribing is a long-term relationship, not a single referral. The client profile organizes intake information, interaction history, and 3-, 6-, and 12-month follow-up stages into one place.

Why it matters

This helps Link Workers understand the client journey before starting a new interaction.

03

Show Client Context Filter

Too much information can slow preparation, but too little information weakens the follow-up call. The context filter lets Link Workers choose what they need to see, such as hobbies, barriers, program attendance, transportation, and communication preferences.

Why it matters

This supports the real workflow of preparing before a call without forcing every detail onto the screen at once.

Client Context Filter
Selectable context categories let workers focus on the details relevant to the next conversation without flattening the full client story.
Log Interaction Workflow
Structured fields, quick tags, narrative notes, and follow-up scheduling work together to keep each interaction usable later.
04

Guided Log Interaction Workflow

Follow-up notes need structure, but older adults’ experiences cannot be reduced to checkboxes. The log interaction workflow combines structured fields with freeform notes, quick tags, and next-step scheduling.

Why it matters

This balances reporting consistency with the need to preserve the client’s story.

05

Contact Attempts and Reschedule Tracking

Follow-up is not always a single successful call. The system supports no answer, rescheduled, discontinued, in-person follow-up, and repeated contact attempts.

Why it matters

Real service workflows include missed calls and changing communication channels, so the system needed to reflect those variations.

Contact Attempt States
Outcome states account for no answer, rescheduled calls, discontinued service, in-person follow-ups, and repeated contact attempts.
Testing / Iteration

Testing changed the way I framed the task

01

The role needed context

Testers needed to understand what a Link Worker does before the task could feel realistic.

02

The task needed a scenario

A vague instruction to “complete the follow-up” did not explain the purpose of the workflow.

03

Context filter needed clearer cause-and-effect

If the layout is not explicit, users may not realize that left-side selections change right-side information.

Before

“Complete the follow-up interaction and save the record.”

After

“You are a Link Worker preparing to call Cora Picard. Review her context, update the interaction log during the call, and save the profile.”

Testing / Iteration Visual
The iteration focused on clearer role framing, a specific client scenario, and more explicit cause-and-effect in the context filter.
Outcome

A clearer structure for long-term follow-up support

What the prototype demonstrated

The prototype clarified the follow-up workflow and demonstrated how structured client context could support Link Workers before, during, and after follow-up interactions.

Expectation vs. reality

I initially thought the system should focus on automation: reminders, priority generation, and recommendations. Through the sprint, I realized that early-stage service tools need to be simple, transparent, and explainable. Link Workers still need human judgement. The system should reduce memory reliance, not replace care work.

What I would improve next

I would test the dashboard and log interaction workflow with real Link Workers, define fixed and centre-specific fields, refine priority logic, and explore AI-assisted notes summarization as a future direction rather than the current core feature.

What I learned

Designing for social care is not just about organizing data. It is about helping people understand the right context at the right moment, so they can make better decisions for the person in front of them.

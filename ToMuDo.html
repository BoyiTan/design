Case Study 03

ToMuDo

AI Task Planner for Turning Overwhelm into Action

When people cannot see what to do first, a task becomes heavier before the work even begins.

ToMuDo explores a mobile AI task planner around task decomposition, visible next steps, time-aware planning, and user autonomy. The goal was not to create another tool to manage, but to help overwhelmed students and workers begin. I was drawn to this problem because school and project pressure often becomes hardest at the moment before starting.

Problem

Complex tasks became psychologically heavy before users started.

Students and workers were not only avoiding work. They were stuck because the next step, workload, and available time felt unclear.

Direction

How might we turn overwhelm into a visible first step?

The design focused on breaking large tasks into understandable actions, then organizing those actions into a plan users could actually begin.

Challenge

Use AI without taking autonomy away.

The planner needed to support users' ability to act, not make them dependent on another system to manage their lives.

Solution Preview

A mobile AI planner that decomposes complex work into ordered steps, generates a todo list, and helps users plan time before they begin.

Input, Breakdown, Todo, Time Plan
Four mobile states show task input, AI decomposition, visible todos, and a time-aware plan.
Research Evidence

The research was about task initiation, not productivity alone.

Our team completed 12 target-user interviews with people who experienced pressure from school, work, and complex tasks. I conducted 3 interviews, contributed theory materials on procrastination and psychological pressure, and coded interview transcripts so the team could translate recurring patterns into wireframing and brainstorming inputs.

Psychological pressure before action

Procrastination was treated as a clarity and readiness problem, not a character flaw or a lack of caring.

Interview-to-wireframe synthesis

Transcript coding helped the team move from broad stories of overwhelm into design opportunities around task breakdown and initiation.

Autonomy as a design requirement

The planner needed to help users understand the work well enough to begin, not make them follow AI blindly.

My Role

My contribution sat between research synthesis and prototype execution.

This was a team project, so my role was not to own every screen end-to-end. I brought theory materials into the second-stage research analysis, conducted 3 of the 12 target-user interviews, analyzed transcripts, and contributed coding that informed team wireframing and brainstorming.

Research and coding

I helped turn interview material and theory about procrastination and psychological pressure into structured design inputs.

Prototype consistency

I participated in the first prototype while the team divided page ownership, with a focus on organizing reusable components and maintaining consistency.

Testing support

The team ran 8 usability tests. I moderated 2 sessions and served as note taker for 2 others.

The Insight

The blocker was not motivation alone.
Users needed a clearer mental model of the task: what it contained, what should happen first, and how the plan fit into the time they had.

Design Response

Designing autonomy before automation

Instead of positioning AI as a decision-maker, the product concept treated AI as a planning scaffold. ToMuDo should reduce the psychological load before task initiation while still leaving the user in control.

01

Turning a large task into visible next steps

Users can start with an overwhelming task or goal, and the AI planner decomposes it into smaller actions. The purpose is to reduce the blank-page feeling before work begins.

Why it matters

A complex task becomes less intimidating when users can see what it contains.

AI Task Decomposition
A large goal is broken into grouped steps so the user can understand what the work contains before starting.
Visible Todo Structure
Ordered todos, completion states, and first-action focus turn the AI breakdown into a usable plan.
02

Generating a todo list users can act on

The decomposed steps become a structured todo list, giving users a clear sequence instead of a vague intention.

Why it matters

The list turns planning into an actionable path, not just another place to store tasks.

03

Planning time around the work

ToMuDo helps users connect steps with time, so they can understand what remains and how the work might fit into their schedule.

Why it matters

Time visibility reduces uncertainty before task initiation.

Time-Aware Plan
Workload blocks and estimated effort make time visible without relying on unsupported outcome metrics.
First-Step Start Flow
The user chooses an adjustable first action instead of managing another complex planner.
04

Helping users start, then step back

The experience should make the first action easy to identify while keeping AI guidance lightweight and adjustable.

Why it matters

Our guiding phrase was: We built this app so users would no longer need it. In the prototype, that meant AI should clarify a first action rather than ask users to manage another system.

Testing / Iteration

Testing focused on whether the plan became actionable

01

Plan comprehension

Could users understand how the AI-generated breakdown related to their original task?

02

Start-point clarity

Could users identify what to do first after reviewing the plan?

03

Autonomy and pressure

Did the planner feel supportive rather than controlling or like another system to maintain?

Outcome

A planner should build the user's ability to begin

What the prototype frames

The prototype frames task planning as a bridge from uncertainty to action. It combines AI task decomposition, visible todos, and time planning so users can understand what to do first.

Expectation vs. reality

I was drawn to this project because I recognized the problem personally: under heavy school and project workload, the hardest part was often the unclear moment before starting. The project shifted my focus from building a powerful planner to designing a scaffold that makes the next step easier to trust.

What I learned

We built this app so users would no longer need it. For me, that phrase describes a design intent: the prototype should help users understand their work, trust their next step, and gradually need less support from the planner.

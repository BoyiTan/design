Case Study 01

ArtFac

Designing Trust in Commission-Based Systems

When users cannot understand how money moves through a creative service, they stop trusting the process.

ArtFac redesigns the custom artwork commission experience through milestone-based payments, transparent progress tracking, and structured artist-client collaboration.

Timeline
8 weeks

UX Research
UI Design

ArtFac Prototype Overview
A project overview showing the commission flow from artist selection to milestone payment, revision, and final delivery.
Problem

Commissioning artwork online often feels uncertain.

Users struggled to understand when to pay, what revisions included, and whether they could trust unfamiliar artists.

Direction

How might we make creative collaboration feel transparent?

The project focused on turning unclear commission stages into visible progress, decisions, and expectations.

Challenge

Balance creative freedom with predictable workflows.

Creative work is flexible by nature, but users still need structure to feel confident moving forward.

Solution Preview

A guided commission system that makes payments, progress, revisions, and approvals visible at the moments users need them most.

The Breakdown

Where understanding broke down

During usability testing, participants frequently skipped or misunderstood key payment information, even when it was technically present in the interface.

“I’m not sure if I pay every time or how it works.”
— Usability Test Participant
“I didn’t notice too much of the payments.”
— Usability Test Participant
The Insight

The issue was not missing information.
Users were surrounded by information—but unable to understand the logic of the system itself.

Design Response

Designing for clarity before completion

Instead of only optimizing task completion, the redesign focused on helping users build a clearer mental model of the commission process.

Make payments visible at every stage
Connect progress to creative milestones
Separate approval from revision decisions
Support trust through structured communication
01

Making progress financially visible

Payments were divided into visible milestones tied directly to creative progress: deposit, sketch approval, development, and final delivery.

Insight

Users needed to see not only what they were paying, but why that payment happened at that moment.

Milestone Payment Flow
The interface ties each payment to a visible creative milestone so clients can understand what they are paying for and why.
Collaboration Status View
A persistent project status layout clarifies completed milestones, active responsibilities, pending approvals, and revision windows.
02

Showing the state of the collaboration

A persistent progress structure communicates the current stage, completed milestones, pending approvals, and revision opportunities.

Insight

Users lost confidence when they could not tell whether the project was waiting on them, the artist, or the system.

03

Structuring revision and feedback decisions

Users can leave feedback, request edits, and review drafts before approval. This keeps collaboration flexible without making the workflow feel ambiguous.

Insight

Strong payment CTAs drew attention away from revision options, so the redesign made decision paths more balanced.

Revision Decision Path
Balanced approval and revision actions make feedback choices visible without letting payment CTAs dominate the workflow.
What Users Revealed

Testing changed what I prioritized

01

Payment information was overlooked

Users focused on moving forward quickly and missed important payment details.

02

Primary actions dominated attention

Strong CTA buttons caused secondary actions such as “Request Edit” to be overlooked.

03

Users wanted more trust signals

Participants expected artist portfolios, previous works, and multiple artist options before selecting.

Outcome

A clearer, more confident commission experience

What changed

The final prototype improved payment visibility, process clarity, and user confidence throughout the commission journey.

Expectation vs. reality

I initially assumed that adding more payment information would solve the trust problem. Testing showed that users did not need more information—they needed the right information to appear with stronger hierarchy at the right moment.

What I learned

Transparency is not simply about providing information. It is about designing when, where, and how important information becomes visible within a user’s decision-making journey.
